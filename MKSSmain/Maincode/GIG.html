GIG WORKERS

"They deliver your food, drive your ride, and power your apps—but who protects their rights?"

Gig workers are individuals who undertake short-term, flexible, or task-based work outside traditional employer-employee relationships. They earn per task or project, often without fixed salaries or long-term contracts.

Types:

  • Platform-Based Gig Workers: Work via digital platforms like Uber, Zomato, Swiggy, Ola, Urban Company, or freelance sites (e.g., Upwork). Examples: ride-hailing drivers, delivery executives, freelancers in IT/design.
  • Non-Platform Gig Workers: Engage in informal, temporary work like construction, event staffing, or seasonal labor, not reliant on digital platforms.

Scale:

Per NITI Aayog (2022), India had ~7.7 million gig workers in 2020-21, projected to reach 23.5 million by 2029-30 (6.7% of non-agricultural workforce). The gig economy contributes ~1.25% to India’s GDP, with a projected value of $455 billion by 2024.

Demographics:

Predominantly young (70% under 40), urban-based, with men dominating mobility/delivery and women in beauty/caregiving roles. Rural migrants often take up non-platform gig work.

Why Are Laws Needed for Gig Workers?

  • Lack of Social Security: No access to insurance, pensions, or paid leave; high personal costs and low net earnings.
  • Job Insecurity: No contracts or protection; fluctuating income based on demand.
  • Algorithmic Exploitation: Platform control via opaque algorithms; unfair penalties.
  • Health and Safety Risks: Frequent injuries without compensation or insurance.
  • Economic Contribution: Drives e-commerce and mobility; needs legal protection.
  • Social Equity: Laws help reduce inequality and protect marginalized communities.

Key Laws for Gig Workers in India (as of July 2025)

  1. National-Level: Code on Social Security, 2020
    • Defines gig and platform workers (Sections 2(35), 2(61)).
    • Social security schemes for health, maternity, pensions (Section 109).
    • Platforms contribute 1-2% of turnover to welfare fund (Section 114).
    • Registration via e-Shram portal with Aadhaar (Section 113).
    • Partially implemented. Rules pending.
  2. State-Level: Rajasthan Platform-Based Gig Workers Act, 2023
    • Mandatory registration for workers and platforms.
    • Welfare Board manages schemes and grievance redressal.
    • Platforms pay up to 2% cess.
    • Transparency in algorithms and pay structures.
    • Operational. Registration ongoing.
  3. Other State Initiatives:
    • Karnataka: Draft Bill under consultation for insurance and rights.
    • Telangana: ₹5 lakh health cover and skill programs.
    • Tamil Nadu & Jharkhand: Exploring welfare boards.
  4. Related Labor Laws:
    • Minimum Wages Act, 1948 — applicable in some contexts.
    • Employees’ State Insurance Act, 1948 — limited coverage.
    • RTI Act, 2005 — to access scheme and fund records.

Challenges and Gaps in Laws

  • Delayed implementation of social security rules.
  • Non-platform gig workers excluded from some laws.
  • Resistance from platforms on cess contributions.
  • Barriers in registration (Aadhaar, literacy).
  • Modest benefits; no paid leave or minimum wage guarantee.
  • Neglect of rural gig workers in most laws.

Judicial and Policy Developments

  • Supreme Court (2023): Ordered government to implement Code on Social Security.
  • Delhi High Court (2024): Clarified non-employee status of gig workers for EPF.
  • NITI Aayog (2022): Suggested contracts, credit, and skill programs.
  • Union Budget 2024-25: Funds allocated for e-Shram platform expansion.

Accessing Official Documentation

  • Code on Social Security: india.gov.in or labour.gov.in
  • Rajasthan Gig Workers Act: rajasthan.gov.in (Legislation)
  • e-Shram Portal: eshram.gov.in
  • State portals: labour.karnataka.gov.in, labour.telangana.gov.in

Sentiment on X

  • Positive: Workers praise Rajasthan’s law for insurance and complaint redressal.
  • Concerns: Platforms fear cost burden; some users report slow rollout.

How to Engage

  • Register on e-Shram or state portals for benefits.
  • File RTI to access scheme records.
  • Join unions like the Indian Federation of App-Based Transport Workers.

Conclusion

Gig workers are a vital but vulnerable segment of India’s labor force. Recent laws like the Code on Social Security and Rajasthan’s Welfare Act attempt to provide protection, but enforcement and inclusion remain key challenges. Continued monitoring, digital access, and RTI use are crucial for equitable reform.
